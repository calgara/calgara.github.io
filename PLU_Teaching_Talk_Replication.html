Using survey & aggregate data to articulate variation in citizen politics.

This Rmarkdown document contains a short tutorial on how to use the Cooperative Congressional Election Study (CCES) and American National Election Study (ANES) to articulate descriptive trends in citizen behavior. I also use scraped (code included for this proecture) aggregate turnout data from Michael McDonald’s (University of Florida) ElectProject to articulate declining voter turnout in presidential and midterm elections. In this tutorial, I include code & output that replicate figures that I use in class. I also provide a brief description of the logic of each figure to articulate a course concept.

1. The Collective Dilemma & Incentive to Free-Ride

Madison posits in Federalist 10 that citizens are motivated by self-interest to form factions that advocate on behalf of their shared interest. However, citizens are not simply motivated by their self-interest and they face a strong incentive to free-ride on the work of other likeminded citizens. If Madison’s theory of human nature is correct, in that:
\[Human Nature \rightarrow_{1} Factions \leftrightarrow_{2} Representation\]
Where: \[\rightarrow_{1} = Self-interest;\] \[\leftrightarrow_{2} = Elections \] than citizens should, in theory, not face collective action problem in forming factions that advocate on their behalf. Thus, if Madison is correct, than citizens would vote in elections at a near universal rate since they will be motivated by a common impulse of passion to go to the polls. Of course, this is not the case given that voting is both a costly form of participation (i.e. intrinsic & information costs) and citizens have a strong incentive to free-ride on the voting behavior of other citizens that share the same collective interest. To articulate that Madison’s theory is incorrect with respect to overcoming the challenge posed by collective action, I make the following figure showing aggregate voter turnout rates in presidential & midterm elections since the Civil War.

2. Who participates in politics?

Given that participation in politics is a costly enterprise, there is inherent variation in the types of citizens that pay this cost. This section uses individual-level data to show variation in citizen participation using various metrics and how citizen resources predict participation in politics on two dimensions: giving political contributions & voting. This section uses data from the 2016 Cooperative Congressional Election Study.

Taken together, there is reason to suspect that political knowledge varies across citizens. What could potentially be the source of this variation? As we discussed, Madison does not posit the dynamic of participation costs in his theory or to what extent do these costs vary by citizens. We could suspect that the capacity to pay participation costs, particularly with respect to the instrict cost of political knowledge, can be influenced by the degree of education held by a citizen. To empirically test this hypothesis, we specify a simple regression model predicting the extent of political knowledge as a function of a citizen’s formal education. The dependent variable used to test this simple bivariate model is the knowledge scale found in Figure 4 & 5 taken from our previous analysis of the American National Election Study. Results of this analysis can be found below.

###### Figure 7: Effect of Education on Political Knowledge ##########

recall$knowledge_scale <- rowSums(recall[,c(2:8)])

recall$educ[recall$educ %in% seq(1,8,1)] <- 1 # > HS
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% 9] <- 2 # HS
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% 10] <- 3 # Some college
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% seq(11,12,1)] <- 4 # 2-yr college
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% 13] <- 5 # BA
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% seq(14,16,1)] <- 6 #Post-BA
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% 90] <- 2
recall$educ[recall$educ %in% 95] <- 2
recall$educ <- factor(recall$educ)

model <- lm(knowledge_scale~educ,data=recall,weights = weight)
coeftest(model, vcov = vcovHC(model, "HC0"))

As one can see, there is a strong non-linear relationship between education and predicted political knowledge. The higher the education a given person posesses, the significantly more likely they are to hold more information about salient political facts in society. For example, the predicted level of political knowledge is approximately 5.5 out of 7 (79%) for those holding a post-graduate degree. By contrast, the predicted level of political knowledge is only 3.9 out of 7 (56%) for those holding a high school diploma. These differences in predicted political knowledge are statistically distinct from one another at \(\rho < 0.001\).

4. Implications for Legislative Representation: Is the Madisonian Model Flawed?

What does this analysis suggest for the nature of institutional legislative representation provided by the Madisonian model of democracy? To assess the implications of variation in the ability to pay participation costs among the American mass public, implications not accounted for by Madison in his ``Theory of the Republic,’’ I specify the following logical exercise. Consider a 900 person congressional district below, comprised of an equal number of citizens (450) being members of a Lutes faction and a Loggers faction. This hypothetical district is visualized below in the following waffle plot.

Would non-universal turnout (i.e., a 100% turnout rate) be a theoritical problem with respect to secureing ``correct’’ and accurate representation where an elective representative represents the views of a majority of their citizens? The data visualization below helps shed some light on this important question.

As the waffle plot above shows, we now have a bias sample of voter turnout with respect to the total population of voters. As one can see (and reflecting the inherently superior political resources garnered by those in the Lutes faction!) 300 of the voters (75%) that showed up are members of the Lutes faction while only 100 of the voters (25%) are members of the Loggers faction. Since there is something systematicly different in the probability of paying the cost to vote between Lutes and Loggers (with Lutes having higher resources to pay these costs!), Lutes are far more likely to vote (50%, to be precise) and will vote for a representative of their faction. Now, why might this be a problem? Let’s assume that Lutes follow the Michigan Model of electoral choice and vote in accordance to their partisan group identity. Naturally, they choose a fellow Lute to represent them and this Lute legislator, acting on the inherent incentives in the representative model of delegation (i.e., Madison’s Theory of the Republic), will vote consistently and 100% in accordance to their principal. In this case, the voters that sent him or her to Capitol Hill, and ultimately who’s vote will be critical for hopes of re-election, are the electoral principals. By contrast, how did Madison envision legislative representative outcomes to look like according to his Theory of the Republic? To shed light on this question, we turn to the specification of his theoritical model in graphical form below.

###### Figure 15: Model of Madisonian Turnout ##########

madison <- data.frame(district=seq(-1,1,0.01),member=seq(-1,1,0.01))

ggplot(madison,aes(x=district,y=member)) + geom_line(size=2) + scale_x_continuous("District Ideology",limits=c(-1,1),breaks=c(-1,0,1),labels=c("-1 \nConservative","0","1 \nLiberal")) + scale_y_continuous("Representative Ideology",limits=c(-1,1),breaks=c(-1,0,1),labels=c("-1 \nConservative","0","1 \nLiberal")) + ggtitle("Madisonian Model of Congressional Representation")

Madison would envision electoral outcomes to follow a perfect one to one relationship between district ideological policy preferences and that of their legislator. Given that voters are tasked with the paramount duty to screen and hold their incumbent legislators accountable, voters will simply toss incumbents out of office that do not reflect aggregate district opinion of the majority of the population/voters. Compounding this dynamic of electoral accountability is the fact that non-incumbent challenger candidates have a strong incentive to be responsive to the concerns of the majority of citizens on the campaign trail and to pick apart an incumbent’s voting record on Capitol Hill. After all, being a member of Congress or a United States Senator is a phenomenal job with incredible related perks! Now, is Madison’s model relaized in electoral outcomes for the U.S. House or U.S. Senate? To do this, I turn to the last set of figures below showing the relationship between district ideological preferences and legislator ideological preferences by political party and legislative chamber. Please note that I use a fancy Aldrich-McKelvey latent variable scaling model to estimate citizen and elite (legislator) preferences on the same scale. For more information on this methodology and its application to assessing critical questions regarding the institutional representation provided to the American mass public by legislative elites, please see Ramey 2015 and Hare et al. 2014.

As one can clearly see, there is strong evidence of a lack of congruence between constituent ideological preferences and those of their elected legislators in the U.S. House and U.S. Senate. There is also clear evidence of leap-frog representation found by Bafumi & Herron 2010. Given the little within-party variation of ideological positions within the Democratic and Republican parties in Congress, the ideological flavor of your elected Democrat or Republican in Congress will be very similar independent of the type of district they represent. For example, the ideological representation provided by a Democratic (Republican) Senator or member of Congress will be essentially the same degree of policy liberal (conservative) if they represent the liberal state of California or the conservative state of Arkansas. Moreover, in the event of a seat turnover in Congress, a relatively conservative Republican will be replaced by a relatively liberal Democrat, providing for ``leap-frog’’ representation and a lack of a significant moderate contingent of legislators. In sum, Madison’s model is not realized in contemporary Congressional election outcomes. If I had the opportunity to teach an entire course of legislative representation, the U.S. Congress, or American political behavior; we would spend much of our time assessing the factors of influencing change in Congressional representation over time and the factors motivating how citizens think of institutional representation and how this influences, ultimately, their electoral behavior in holding elites accountable for what they do in office!
